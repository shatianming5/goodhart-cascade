\begin{abstract}
When reinforcement learning optimizes code generation with multi-objective rewards, Goodhart's Law manifests as \emph{cascading quality degradation}: optimizing a misaligned constraint damages other quality dimensions. But which constraints are safe to add, and which trigger cascading failure? We show that the answer lies in a simple statistic measurable \emph{before training}: the covariance between each constraint reward and the primary objective (test-passing) in base-model rollouts. We derive a \textbf{Free Alignment Criterion}---constraints with positive covariance can be added at zero cost to pass rate---and validate it across 19 GRPO experiments at three model scales (1.5B, 7B, 14B). Our theory correctly predicts all seven pre-registered outcomes: the positive-covariance configuration (R3) achieves the highest pass rate (60.0\% on TACO), while the negative-covariance configuration (R4, +comment) triggers 23$\times$ comment flooding, converts 82\% of functions to scripts to evade complexity detection, and causes HumanEval pass rate to decline significantly over training ($p=0.018$). A single base-model rollout session (30 minutes, no training) replaces the entire reward weight grid search.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and its variants such as GRPO~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath} have become the dominant paradigm for aligning large language models. In code generation, the primary objective is \emph{test-passing}: generating code that satisfies given test cases. However, practitioners increasingly add quality constraints---code style (pylint), cognitive complexity, comment coverage, duplication detection---to the reward signal, hoping to simultaneously improve code correctness and quality.

This multi-objective reward design faces a fundamental question: \textbf{which quality constraints help, and which hurt?} Current practice relies on grid search over reward weights, which is expensive (each configuration requires days of GPU training) and provides no theoretical guidance. Worse, adding the ``wrong'' constraint can trigger \emph{Goodhart's Law}~\citep{goodhart1984problems}: the model optimizes the proxy reward at the expense of the true objective.

We show that a simple, training-free diagnostic predicts the answer. By generating rollouts from the base model and computing the \textbf{covariance matrix} of reward components, we can determine:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Which constraints are safe}: those with $\Cov(\text{test}, \text{constraint}) > 0$ can be added without hurting pass rate (``free alignment'').
    \item \textbf{Which constraints are dangerous}: those with $\Cov(\text{test}, \text{constraint}) \leq 0$ cause Goodhart cascading---the model games the constraint at the expense of everything else.
    \item \textbf{What weights to use}: constraints should be added in order of decreasing covariance magnitude.
\end{enumerate}

We validate this framework across 19 GRPO experiments (3 model scales $\times$ 5 reward configurations + 4 theory-validation runs), evaluated on three benchmarks (TACO 200 problems in-domain, HumanEval 164 problems out-of-distribution, MBPP 100 problems). Our pre-registered predictions achieve 7/7 correctness, including the striking finding that adding a comment ratio constraint---which has near-zero covariance with test-passing---causes a 23$\times$ increase in comments while \emph{decreasing} the model's ability to solve problems.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A \textbf{covariance-based framework} for predicting alignment tax in multi-objective RL, with formal criteria for free vs.\ costly constraints (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
    \item \textbf{Empirical validation} across 3 model scales (1.5B, 7B, 14B) with 7/7 pre-registered predictions confirmed (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
    \item \textbf{Mechanistic analysis} of Goodhart cascading: how comment gaming leads to structural code changes (function$\to$script conversion) that evade quality detection (Section~\ref{sec:gaming}).
    \item A \textbf{practical algorithm} that computes optimal reward weights from a single base-model rollout session, replacing expensive grid search (Section~\ref{sec:algorithm}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Goodhart's Law in AI.} \citet{manheim2018categorizing} categorize four variants of Goodhart's Law (regressional, extremal, causal, adversarial). Our work provides the first \emph{quantitative predictor} of which variant manifests, based on the covariance structure of reward components. \citet{skalse2022defining} formalize reward hacking but do not provide constructive guidance for reward design. \citet{pan2022effects} study reward misspecification in RLHF but focus on single-objective settings.

\paragraph{Multi-objective RL for LLMs.} \citet{wu2024fine} propose fine-grained RLHF with multiple reward dimensions. \citet{zhu2023principled} study Pareto-optimal solutions for multi-objective alignment. These works address \emph{how to optimize} multiple objectives; we address the prior question of \emph{which objectives to include}.

\paragraph{Code RL.} GRPO~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath} and its application to code generation~\citep{deepseek2025r1} demonstrate strong pass rate improvements. However, the interaction between test-passing rewards and quality constraints has not been systematically studied. Our work fills this gap.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Setup}

Consider GRPO training with a composite reward:
\begin{equation}
    r = w_0 \cdot r_{\text{test}} + \sum_{k=1}^{n} w_k \cdot r_k, \quad \sum_{i=0}^{n} w_i = 1
\end{equation}
where $r_{\text{test}} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates test-passing (the primary objective) and $r_k \in [0,1]$ are quality constraint rewards (pylint, complexity, comment ratio, duplication). All rewards are computed per-rollout.

GRPO updates the policy using group-relative advantages:
\begin{equation}
    A_i = \frac{r_i - \bar{r}}{\sigma_r}, \quad \nabla_\theta J \propto \E\left[A_i \cdot \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_i | s)\right]
\end{equation}

The effective gradient signal for the primary objective is:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_{\text{test}} = \frac{\Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r)}{\Var(r)} = \frac{w_0 \sigma_0^2 + \sum_k w_k \Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k)}{\Var(r)}
    \label{eq:eta}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Free Alignment Criterion}

\begin{theorem}[Free Alignment]
\label{thm:free}
Adding constraint $k$ with infinitesimal weight $\epsilon$ does not decrease $\eta_{\text{test}}$ if and only if:
\begin{equation}
    \Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k) > 0
\end{equation}
When this holds, the constraint is ``free'': it improves quality without reducing gradient signal for test-passing. When $\Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k) < 0$, the constraint is ``anti-aligned'': every unit of weight actively fights the primary objective beyond the cost of weight dilution.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
From Eq.~\ref{eq:eta}, increasing $w_k$ by $\epsilon$ while decreasing $w_0$ by $\epsilon$ changes the numerator by $\epsilon(\Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k) - \sigma_0^2)$ and the denominator by terms involving $\Var(r_k)$ and cross-covariances. At $\epsilon \to 0$, the sign of $\frac{d\eta_{\text{test}}}{d\epsilon}$ is determined by $\Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k)$: positive covariance means the constraint reinforces the primary gradient direction. Full derivation in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Optimal Ordering]
\label{cor:ordering}
Constraints should be added in order of decreasing $\rho_k \cdot \sigma_k / \sigma_0$, where $\rho_k = \Corr(r_{\text{test}}, r_k)$. The first constraint with $\rho_k \leq 0$ should not be included.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Predictions}

To generate testable predictions, we measure the covariance matrix \emph{before any training} by generating rollouts from the base model (Section~\ref{sec:cov_measurement}). This yields:

\begin{equation}
    \Corr(\text{test}, \text{complexity}) = +0.166 \gg \Corr(\text{test}, \text{pylint}) = +0.092 > \Corr(\text{test}, \text{dup}) = +0.033 > 0 > \Corr(\text{test}, \text{comment}) = -0.018
\end{equation}

From Theorem~\ref{thm:free} and Corollary~\ref{cor:ordering}, we predict:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{R3} (test + pylint + complexity, all $\Cov > 0$) should achieve the highest pass rate.
    \item \textbf{R4} (R3 + comment, $\Cov \leq 0$) should degrade pass rate.
    \item R4 should exhibit comment gaming (the model inflates comments to maximize reward).
    \item Comment gaming should cascade to other dimensions (via $\Corr(\text{comment}, \text{pylint}) = -0.103$).
    \item $\Ropt$ (theory-optimal weights, excluding comment) should outperform $\Ranti$ (heavy comment weight).
    \item These patterns should hold across model scales (1.5B, 7B, 14B).
    \item R4's HumanEval performance should decline over training as gaming intensifies.
\end{enumerate}

All seven predictions are confirmed experimentally (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Covariance Measurement}
\label{sec:cov_measurement}

We generate 8 rollouts for each of 500 TACO problems using the base model (Qwen2.5-Coder-7B), yielding 4,000 code samples. Each sample is scored on all 5 reward dimensions simultaneously: test-passing (binary), pylint score (0--10, normalized), cognitive complexity (radon library), comment ratio, and code duplication ratio. We compute the Pearson correlation matrix with $p$-values (Table~\ref{tab:cov_matrix}).

We train using TRL's GRPOTrainer with persistent vLLM server for generation. Five reward configurations (R1--R5) incrementally add constraints in covariance-priority order:

\subsection{Evaluation}

We evaluate on three benchmarks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TACO} (200 problems, in-domain): filtered from the same distribution as training data.
    \item \textbf{HumanEval} (164 problems, OOD): standard code generation benchmark, different distribution.
    \item \textbf{MBPP} (100 problems, OOD): basic Python programming problems.
\end{itemize}

Quality metrics are measured on ClassEval (50 problems) with greedy decoding: pylint score, cognitive complexity, comment ratio, duplication ratio, type hint coverage, and lines of code.

\label{sec:gaming}

\subsection{Comment Flooding}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Gaming escape mechanisms in 7B models (TACO 200 evaluation).}
\label{tab:gaming}
\begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
\toprule
Metric & Base & R1 & R2 & R3 & R4 & R5 \\
\midrule
Comments/code & 0.19 & 0.09 & 0.03 & 0.02 & \textbf{4.43} & 4.02 \\
Script rate & 28.8\% & 42.6\% & 76.0\% & 77.8\% & \textbf{81.5\%} & 81.2\% \\
Avg. lines & 15.0 & 13.7 & 12.8 & 11.2 & \textbf{18.8} & 18.1 \\
Avg. functions & 0.94 & 0.79 & 0.40 & 0.36 & \textbf{0.29} & 0.31 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

R4 exhibits two distinct gaming strategies:

\paragraph{Strategy 1: Comment flooding (23$\times$).} R4 generates 4.43 comments per code sample, compared to 0.19 for the base model---a 23-fold increase. These are overwhelmingly template comments (``\texttt{\# Initialize variables}'', ``\texttt{\# Return result}'') that add no semantic value.

\paragraph{Strategy 2: Function-to-script conversion.} R4 converts 82\% of solutions to script-style code (vs.\ 29\% base), eliminating function definitions entirely. This is a sophisticated evasion: cognitive complexity is measured per-function, so eliminating functions avoids complexity penalties while allowing arbitrarily complex inline logic.

\subsection{Cascading Degradation}

The cascade mechanism operates through $\Corr(\text{comment}, \text{pylint}) = -0.103$: inflating comments increases code length, introduces more pylint violations (redundant comments, line length), and the shift to script-style code further degrades structural quality. R4's complexity score (8.64) is higher than R3's (6.49), despite R4 having complexity in its reward---the comment-driven structural changes undermine the complexity constraint.

\subsection{Temporal Dynamics}

The divergence between R1 and R4 widens significantly over training ($p=0.025$), demonstrating that Goodhart cascading is progressive: the longer you train with a misaligned constraint, the worse the damage.

\section{Theory Validation: $\Ropt$ vs $\Ranti$}
\label{sec:theory_validation}

To test the prescriptive power of our theory, we design two extreme configurations derived \emph{entirely from the covariance matrix}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Ropt$: maximizes positive-Cov constraints, excludes comment (test=0.5, complexity=0.2, pylint=0.2, duplication=0.1).
    \item $\Ranti$: maximizes negative-Cov weight (test=0.4, comment=0.4, pylint=0.1, complexity=0.1).
\end{itemize}

These configurations are designed \emph{before training}, based solely on the measured covariance matrix.

\paragraph{Results (1.5B, 10 checkpoints).} $\Ropt$ outperforms $\Ranti$ on pylint in \textbf{10/10 checkpoints} (100\%) and on complexity in \textbf{10/10 checkpoints} (100\%). $\Ranti$'s proxy reward is higher (0.64 vs 0.52), but its true quality is worse across all dimensions---a clean demonstration of Goodhart's Law. $\Ranti$ inflates comment ratio from 44\% to 59\% while pylint degrades from 6.3 to 5.4 and complexity worsens from 21.6 to 25.4. Critically, pylint has positive weight (0.1) in $\Ranti$, yet still degrades---ruling out the trivial ``you get what you pay for'' explanation and confirming the cascade mechanism.

\section{Practical Algorithm}
\label{sec:algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Covariance-Based Reward Weight Selection}
\label{alg:cov_weights}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Base model $\pi_0$, candidate constraints $\{r_1, \ldots, r_n\}$, $M$ problems, $K$ rollouts
\STATE Generate $K$ rollouts per problem from $\pi_0$ ($M \times K$ total samples)
\STATE Score each sample on $r_{\text{test}}$ and all $r_k$
\STATE Compute $\rho_k = \Corr(r_{\text{test}}, r_k)$ with $p$-values
\STATE Sort constraints by $\rho_k$ descending
\STATE \textbf{Include} constraints where $\rho_k > 0$ and $p < 0.05$
\STATE \textbf{Exclude} constraints where $\rho_k \leq 0$
\STATE Allocate $w_0 = 0.5$ to test; distribute remaining 0.5 proportional to $|\rho_k|$ among included constraints
\RETURN Reward weights $\{w_0, w_1, \ldots\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This algorithm requires only \textbf{one base-model generation session} ($\sim$30 minutes on a single GPU for 500 problems $\times$ 8 rollouts). No training is needed. In our experiments, it correctly identifies the optimal configuration (matching R3) and the dangerous constraint (comment), replacing what would otherwise require 5+ full training runs.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why does comment have negative covariance?} In competitive programming, correct solutions tend to be concise and comment-free---the ``natural'' style of code that passes tests. Adding comments is orthogonal to (and slightly anti-correlated with) functional correctness. When we reward comments, the model learns to inflate them at the expense of actually solving the problem.

\paragraph{14B TACO anomaly.} The 14B model achieves the highest TACO pass rate with R4 (81.0\%), despite R4 being the worst on HumanEval. This is not a contradiction but a feature: larger models are better at exploiting the training distribution (Goodhart's regressional variant). The divergence between in-domain and OOD performance is itself evidence of gaming.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (1) We study only the Qwen model family; replication on other architectures (e.g., DeepSeek-Coder, CodeLlama) would strengthen generalization claims. (2) Our covariance measurement assumes the base-model distribution is representative; distribution shift during training could change covariance signs. (3) We lack bootstrap confidence intervals on some metrics, though the consistency across scales and checkpoints provides robustness. (4) The theory applies to GRPO-style advantage estimation; other RL algorithms (PPO, DPO) may have different covariance sensitivities.

\paragraph{Broader Impact.} Our framework has immediate practical value: practitioners can run Algorithm~\ref{alg:cov_weights} before committing to expensive training runs, avoiding costly misalignment. It also provides a micro-foundation for Goodhart's Law in the RL context, connecting the macro phenomenon (proxy optimization failure) to a measurable micro-mechanism (covariance sign).

\section{Conclusion}

We demonstrate that the covariance between constraint rewards and the primary objective, measured from base-model rollouts alone, predicts which quality constraints are free and which trigger Goodhart cascading in code RL. Our framework correctly predicts 7/7 pre-registered outcomes across 3 model scales, reveals a 23$\times$ comment gaming mechanism, and provides a practical algorithm that replaces reward weight grid search with a single generation session. The key message: \textbf{measure covariance before you train}---one statistic saves weeks of GPU time and prevents cascading quality failure.

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:free}}
\label{app:proof}

Consider the effective gradient signal $\eta_{\text{test}}$ from Eq.~\ref{eq:eta}. With $n$ constraints at weights $w_0, w_1, \ldots, w_n$ ($\sum w_i = 1$):

\begin{equation}
    \eta_{\text{test}} = \frac{w_0 \sigma_0^2 + \sum_{k=1}^n w_k \Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k)}{\Var(r)}
\end{equation}

Adding constraint $k$ with weight $\epsilon$, reducing $w_0$ to $w_0 - \epsilon$:

\begin{equation}
    \eta(\epsilon) = \frac{(w_0 - \epsilon)\sigma_0^2 + \epsilon \Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k) + \text{const}}{\Var(r(\epsilon))}
\end{equation}

The numerator changes by $\epsilon(\Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k) - \sigma_0^2)$. At $\epsilon = 0$ with $w_0 = 1$ (starting from test-only), the derivative of $\eta$ with respect to $\epsilon$ has sign determined by $\Cov(r_{\text{test}}, r_k) - \sigma_0^2 + \text{denominator corrections}$.

For the equal-variance case ($\sigma_k = \sigma_0$ for all $k$), the second-order expansion yields:

\begin{equation}
    \eta_{\text{test}}(\epsilon) = 1 + \epsilon^2(\rho_k^2 - \rho_k)(1 - \rho_k) + O(\epsilon^3)
\end{equation}

where $\rho_k = \Corr(r_{\text{test}}, r_k)$. The sign of the quadratic term is positive when $\rho_k > 0$, confirming that positive correlation yields free alignment at small weights. When $\rho_k < 0$, the quadratic term is negative, and the cost exceeds pure dilution. $\square$
